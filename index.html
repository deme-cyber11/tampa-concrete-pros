That Crack Isn't Going Away on Its Own

You've been watching it since last winter. Maybe it started as a hairline, maybe it was always a little rough — but now it's wide enough to catch a heel, high enough to scrape a bumper, or bad enough that you think twice every time someone walks across it.

Cracked, heaving, and crumbling concrete is the most common exterior problem Tampa homeowners call us about — and it's almost always worse underneath the surface than it looks from above. A crack you can see means movement has already happened. A heaved section means the base has failed or tree roots are winning.

In Tampa's climate — heavy summer rains, rapid soil saturation, and root systems that grow year-round — concrete that looks like it's "just old" may actually be a liability waiting for the wrong day. If this is urgent: (813) 705-9021.

What Waiting Actually Costs You

The instinct is to wait — it's a big project, it feels disruptive, you're not sure what it'll cost. But here's what waiting does:

Water infiltration. Every crack that opens is a channel for Tampa's heavy rains to get under the slab. Florida's sandy soil erodes fast, and once the base is undermined the crack opens further every season.
Base failure. Once the base is compromised, you're not just replacing the concrete — you're also paying to excavate, re-compact, and regrade. That adds $1–$2/sq ft to every repair you delay.
Trip hazard liability. If a guest, delivery driver, or neighbor trips on your driveway or walkway, you own that liability. A heaved section is a documented hazard.
Resale consequences. Tampa real estate agents consistently report that exterior concrete in rough shape delays sales and invites lowball offers. Fixing it under pressure costs more than fixing it on your schedule.
Limited contractor availability. Tampa concrete contractors book out 3–4 weeks in spring and fall. If you wait until a sale is imminent, you may be waiting longer than you can afford.

What Does a New Driveway Cost in Tampa?

Most contractors won't post pricing. We do — because surprises at signing help no one.

Concrete removal (if replacing existing): $1–$2/sq ft including haul-off. Standard two-car driveway (~600 sq ft): $600–$1,200 to demo.
New driveway installation: $6–$12/sq ft (labor, materials, base prep). Standard two-car driveway (~600 sq ft): $3,600–$7,200.
Stamped and decorative concrete: $12–$20/sq ft. Patterns, textures, and integral color during finishing. More visual impact, more labor.
Concrete patio or pool deck: $4–$8/sq ft depending on size, finish, and elevation changes.
Mudjacking / concrete leveling: $500–$2,500. Only appropriate when the slab is structurally intact but settled — not when it's cracked, spalled, or failing.

Always included in our quotes: base prep, reinforcement, forming, finishing, and haul-off. No surprise line items.

Tampa's sandy soil and heavy summer rain make drainage a structural issue, not just an aesthetic one. Every pour we do includes a slope check so water moves away from the slab, not under it. That's what separates a driveway that lasts 20 years from one that's cracking in 3.
